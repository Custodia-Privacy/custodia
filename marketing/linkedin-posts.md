## POST 1 — Problem/Insight (GDPR Misconception)

A cookie banner is not GDPR compliance.

I say this because I've talked to dozens of founders who think slapping a "We use cookies" popup on their site means they're covered. They're not. Not even close.

Here's what GDPR actually requires:

— A privacy policy that lists every data processor you use (Stripe, Intercom, GA4, Hotjar... all of them)
— A lawful basis for every type of data you collect
— A way for users to request, export, or delete their data
— Records showing you actually manage this stuff

The cookie banner? That's just the very first layer — and most of them are implemented wrong anyway (pre-ticked boxes are illegal).

I built Custodia specifically because I was frustrated by how misleading the "just add a banner" advice is. Small businesses are genuinely exposed, and they don't know it.

## POST 4 — Stat/Insight (State Privacy Laws)

15 US states now have active privacy laws. Most small businesses don't know which ones apply to them.

California (CCPA/CPRA) gets all the press. But Texas, Virginia, Colorado, Connecticut, Oregon, Montana, Florida, Delaware, Iowa, Indiana, Tennessee, New Hampshire, New Jersey, and Maryland have all passed their own versions.

They're not identical. The thresholds differ. The consumer rights differ. Some have opt-out requirements for selling data, some require data protection assessments, some have cure periods, some don't.

If your website gets traffic from US users — and almost every website does — the patchwork is genuinely complicated.

The thing is, most of these laws have similar core requirements: disclose what data you collect, give users a way to opt out, don't sell data without consent. Getting the fundamentals right covers a lot of ground across jurisdictions.

## POST 5 — Story / Anecdote

A company I know got a GDPR complaint that cost €15,000 in legal fees.

Their "compliance" was a free privacy policy template they'd copied from another website.

Here's what happened: a user in the EU requested a copy of all the data the company held on them — a standard DSAR (Data Subject Access Request). The company had no process for this. They weren't even sure which systems held user data.

They engaged a law firm. Two months of back-and-forth, document review, drafting a proper response. The complaint didn't go to a regulator and there was no fine — but the legal bill was €15k. For a 12-person company, that's a serious hit.

The template policy failed them because it listed data practices they didn't follow and omitted processors they actually used. It was worse than having nothing — it was evidence of negligence.

This is exactly the problem with copy-paste compliance. A real privacy policy has to reflect your actual tech stack: which payment processor you use, which email platform, which analytics tool, which CRM. Templates can't do that.
